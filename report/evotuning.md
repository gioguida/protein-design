**Evotuned ESM2 \+ DPO for Low-Data Antibody Fitness Prediction**  
   
**What we've observed so far**  
   
We tested this on the C05 antibody DMS dataset, which has around 200k sequences spanning ED1 to ED11 (edit distance from the wild type). We only used ED1–5 (ED2\_m22 Dataset) for DPO training and held out higher edit-distance datasets test sets, so the model never sees anything past ED5 during training.  
   
We compared two setups: a vanilla ESM2-35M trained directly with DPO, and an ESM2-35M that we first evotuned (kept training with MLM) on OAS before running DPO. For the evotuning step we tried a few ways of curating the OAS data: the full corpus, filtering for sequences similar to C05's wild type, and focusing on CDR regions.  
   
Evotuning helps a lot, especially in the low-N regime. The gap is biggest on ED5 where the vanilla model's zero-shot score is actually negative. The three evotuning curation variants performed about the same as each other, so how we curated the OAS data doesn't seem to matter much here.  
   
The natural next question is whether this holds up on other antibody DMS datasets, not just C05. We'll start simple: full-OAS evotune vs. vanilla, with no WT-similarity filtering, and only bring back the curation variants (CDR-mix, etc.) afterward if the basic result generalizes.  
   
**Related works**  
   
Here's the closest published work I found, and how it differs from what we're doing:  
   
\- The "evotuning" concept itself (unsupervised fine-tuning of a pretrained sequence model on the evolutionary neighborhood of a target protein, before low-N supervised training) originates in Biswas et al., 2021, \*Nature Methods\*, "Low-N protein engineering with data-efficient deep learning" \[https://www.nature.com/articles/s41592-021-01100-y — Results, "A paradigm for low-N protein engineering": they describe fine-tuning UniRep on sequences evolutionarily related to the target protein (eUniRep), then training a simple supervised top model on a low-N number of characterized mutants\]. This validated the idea on two very different proteins (avGFP, TEM-1 β-lactamase) to test generalization, same spirit as our next step, but with UniRep \+ linear regression, not ESM2 \+ DPO, and not on antibodies specifically.  
   
\- DPO applied to protein language models is established via Widatalla et al. (ProteinDPO), "Aligning protein generative models with experimental fitness via Direct Preference Optimization" \[https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.1101/2024.05.20.595026v1.full — Introduction: they frame an "alignment gap" where unsupervised pretraining doesn't guarantee alignment with the function of interest, and use DPO on a structure-conditioned model (ESM-IF1) trained on the Megascale stability dataset\]. This is stability prediction, not antibody-antigen binding, and does not involve an evotuning stage before DPO.  
   
\- g-DPO \[https://arxiv.org/pdf/2510.19474 — Abstract/Methods: their pipeline explicitly begins with "unsupervised fine-tuning of a PLM on evolutionarily related sequences of a wild type protein (evo-tuning)" followed by DPO, validated on three protein mutational landscapes\] is the closest published combination of evotuning \+ DPO. Their contribution is about training efficiency (sequence clustering \+ union masking to reduce redundant pairwise comparisons).  
   
\- Talaei, Walker et al. (2025), "CDR-aware masked language models for paired antibodies enable state-of-the-art binding prediction" \[https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.1101/2025.10.31.685149v1.full.pdf\]. Full detail in Section 3.2 below. This paper does not use DPO at all (uses frozen embeddings \+ ridge regression), and works on paired VH/VL sequences. It also does not test a low-N supervised-training curve, their downstream evaluation is 10-fold CV on the full available dataset per antigen, not a data-scaling curve.  
   
\- Hawkins-Hooker et al. (2024), "Likelihood-based fine-tuning of protein language models for few-shot fitness prediction and design" \[https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.1101/2024.05.28.596156v4 — Section 5.1 "Low-n fitness prediction": they fine-tune PLM likelihoods directly with a Bradley-Terry pairwise-ranking loss over sequence pairs, tested at n \= 24, 32, 48, 128, 512 sequences per ProteinGym dataset\]. This is close to DPO in spirit but simpler, and they explicitly tried and dropped the DPO-style KL regularization term, since their setting is scoring rather than generation \[same source, Appendix B.2, "Relationship to preference learning for LLMs": "in our own experiments we did not find this regularisation necessary to achieve good performance. We hypothesise that this is because we do not require generations from the model, unlike typical applications of DPO."\]. No evotuning stage, no antibody data, and no antibody-specific low-N generalization test.  
   
   
**Design decisions:**  
   
**OAS filtering, download, deduplication, and splits**  
Download from OAS with only filters: human race and chain heavy.  
For filtering, I'm following the AbLang-2 pipeline pretty closely \[Olsen, Moal & Deane, 2024, §2.1: "The sequences were then filtered by removing duplicates, sequences missing conserved cysteines, and heavily fragmented (missing \>16 residues from the N-terminus or 7 residues from the C-terminus) sequences. Unpaired sequences were additionally filtered to remove sequences only seen once. Finally, any amino acids other than the standard 20 were changed to X."\]. Concretely, applied in order:  
   
\-       Remove duplicate sequences (by exact match).  
\-       Remove sequences missing the conserved cysteines that form the canonical disulfide bond in the Ig fold.  
\-       Remove heavily fragmented sequences, specifically those missing more than 16 residues from the N-terminus or more than 7 residues from the C-terminus.  
\-       Replace any non-standard amino acid with an X token rather than dropping the sequence.  
   
\[Talaei et al., whose focus was antibody property prediction for therapeutics, filter differently: they restrict by human species and exclude autoimmune-disease repertoires at the download stage. {DECIDED NOT TO DO}.\]  
   
For deduplication, I want to move away from our current MMseqs2-at-99%-identity setup, toward either the AbLang-2 or Talaei et al. approach. AbLang-2 clusters in two stages: first by identical CDR3, then within each group by 95% whole-sequence identity via Linclust, keeping the longest sequence in each cluster as the representative rather than an arbitrary one \["Unpaired sequences were clustered first based on identical CDR3s and thereafter by 95% identity over the whole sequence using Linclust \[Steinegger and Söding 2018\] with –cov-mode 1\. \[...\] This cluster fragments together with a longer representative sequence." — same source, §2.1\]. Talaei et al. do it in one stage instead: cluster by 95% sequence identity only, and keep one representative per cluster \[Talaei, Walker et al., 2025 — Methods §4.1.1: "We clustered the remaining antibody sequences with Linclust for the unpaired pretraining corpus and MMSeq2 for the paired finetuning corpus at a 95% sequence identity threshold to remove near duplicates between training and validation."\].  
Both use Linclust rather than MMseqs2, and for good reason: MMseqs2 is more sensitive but much slower, closer to super-linear scaling, while Linclust runs in linear time and trades a bit of sensitivity for roughly 30–60x faster runtime \[Steinegger & Söding, 2018, \*Nature Communications\*, "Clustering huge protein sequence sets in linear time" — https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-018-04964-5\]. That matters for us too: dropping some of our current filters will likely increase the corpus size a lot, to the point where MMseqs2 alone becomes infeasible.  
For the split, I'm following Talaei, Walker et al. rather than AbLang-2. AbLang-2's split logic is built around their paired test set: they cluster the unpaired pool together with a held-out paired test set and remove any unpaired sequence landing in the same cluster, to avoid leaking into the paired benchmark. That doesn't apply to us since we have no paired data. Talaei et al.'s approach is simpler and fits better: after clustering the full corpus at 95% identity and keeping one representative per cluster, that pool of representatives is the deduplicated dataset, and validation is just a random subsample of it, "the unpaired pretraining set consists of 1,220,072,064 cluster representatives, from which 12,200,720 sequences are selected for validation" \[Talaei, Walker et al., 2025, §4.1.1\] (almost exactly 1%). This prevents near-duplicate leakage across the split by construction, and we can build the test split the same way.

Follow Talaei, Walker et al.'s unpaired pretraining ablation, since it's the same objective (continued MLM on unpaired OAS): AdamW, learning rate 1×10⁻⁵, warmup ratio 0.05, weight decay 0.01, run for a single epoch over their unpaired corpus \[Talaei, Walker et al., 2025 — Methods §4.2.3: "AdamW with learning rate 1×10−5, warmup ratio 0.05, and weight decay 0.01"\].  
   
Why are we staying single-chain (unpaired), for now? In the CDR-aware masking paper they pretrain ESM2 on single-chain OAS with whole-chain masking, then fine-tune on paired H-L data, and compare that against going straight from base to paired fine-tuning \[Talaei, Walker et al., 2025 — Results §2.2.3, "Unpaired pretraining ablation"\]. The unpaired stage on its own clearly does something: it gives "a large jump over the released base model across regions, confirming that single-chain data are informative" \[same source, §2.2.3\]. But once paired fine-tuning is added on top, that head start disappears: paired-only training from base matches or slightly beats the unpaired-then-paired route, both on masked-recovery accuracy (Fig. 2E/F) and on downstream binding prediction (Table S1). In their own words: "the extra unpaired stage provides no measurable benefit for masked recovery (and did not improve downstream binding in our experiments...) while adding substantial compute and pipeline complexity" \[same source, §2.2.3\]. Their explanation: "it did not translate into downstream binding gains in our trials, suggesting representation drift: single-chain optimization can over-weight framework regularities and germline/frequency biases that are weakly predictive of pair-dependent binding" \[same source, Discussion (§3)\]. This finding is specifically about their setup, where the final representation is always paired (VH+VL concatenated, jointly pooled). Our downstream task never uses a paired representation at all, our DMS dataset only varies the heavy chain, so this doesn't tell us unpaired evotuning is useless in general, only that it's redundant once paired fine-tuning is added on top. Since our downstream task is single-chain by construction, unpaired evotuning is the matched choice for us.  
For now we're sticking with unpaired-only training. If a paired ablation turns out to be worth doing, we can add it later, but the more useful ablation right now is on the unpaired side: what happens as we subsample (chosen to do random subsampling) the unpaired OAS data down to different sizes.   
   
**Masking**  
The CDR-aware masking paper compares a few different masking strategies for the fine-tuning stage, across two backbones (ESM2-3B and ESM C, 600M). For ESM2-3B they implemented a two-stage curriculum: Stage I trains with whole-chain masking (WC-15%) for up to 10 epochs, then Stage II branches off from a Stage I checkpoint (epoch 5\) and continues training under either CDR-focused or Hybrid masking (each strategy better defined below) \[Talaei, Walker et al., 2025 — Methods §4.2.4: "We adapt ESM2-3B to paired heavy–light sequences in two stages: Stage I (WC) uses whole chain masking for up to 10 epochs...Stage II branches from Stage I WC and is run under two objectives: (i) CDR masking at 50% within annotated CDRs; and (ii) Hybrid masking with an 80% CDR / 20% WC mixture per batch."\]. {STILL OPEN: the paper never explains why 10 epochs, or why epoch 5 specifically — no stopping rule is given for this part of the pipeline. How should we decide our own equivalent intermediate stopping point?} So for ESM2, "CDR-focused" and "Hybrid" both mean whole-chain-warmup-then-something, not CDR-only training from the base model. ESM C (600M) is set up a bit differently: it can also train CDR-50% directly from the base checkpoint, skipping the whole-chain warmup entirely \[same source, §4.2.4\]. The paper doesn't explain this difference directly, but the likely reason is compute: ESM2-3B is much larger than ESM C, so running every masking policy from scratch on both models probably wasn't affordable, and the two-stage curriculum was the practical compromise for the bigger backbone. That constraint doesn't apply to us, for the moment, since we're working with a much smaller model (ESM2-35M), so we can just ablate all of the alternatives directly: whole-chain-only, CDR-only, Hybrid, and our own masking strategy, each trained on its own rather than bolted onto a warmup stage. The masking policies themselves are defined as:  
\-       Whole-chain (WC-15%): standard BERT-style masking, 15% of residues selected uniformly at random, with the 80/10/10 mask/random/keep split.  
\-       CDR-focused (CDR-50%): masking applied only within annotated CDR loops, at 50% (chosen to match the absolute number of masked residues as the 15% whole-chain scheme, for fair comparison).  
\-       Hybrid: 80% CDR masking \+ 20% whole-chain masking per batch, intended to reduce the risk of catastrophic forgetting of non-CDR (framework) positions.  
   
On top of these, we'll also ablate our own current masking strategy against the paper's three regimes: for each CDR position, mask it one at a time (instead of masking a whole 50% chunk of the CDR at once, like their CDR-focused regime does), plus a few flanking positions just outside the CDR, plus a small number of random framework positions. The idea is that masking one position at a time trains the model on exactly the conditional distribution, the probability of the residue at that position given the rest of the sequence, that's what gets read off at scoring time for a single point mutation (mask the mutated position, and get the log-probability of the true amino acid). So this masking strategy should be the closest match to how we actually score single mutants, which is most of what we'll test on early. {STILL OPEN: right now we split C05 into "single mutant" vs "everything else", should that instead be single mutant vs strictly multi-mutant (\>1 mutation), for a cleaner comparison?}  
   
\[https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.1101/2025.10.31.685149v1.full.pdf — Methods §4.2.2 "Masking policies" for the exact definitions above; §4.2.4 for the Stage I/Stage II training pipeline; Results §2.3.1-2.3.2 for the downstream comparison\]  
   
Key results relevant to us:  
\-       CDR-focused fine-tuning produced consistently stronger embeddings for downstream binding-affinity regression than whole-chain fine-tuning, with reported R2 improvements up to \~27% on some combinatorial-mutant benchmarks, across three antigen systems with different binding modes (SARS-CoV-2 spike HR2 region, fluorescein, hemagglutinin) \[same source, Results §2.3.2\]. The exact checkpoint differs per backbone, though: for ESM2-3B it's the Stage II CDR branch at epoch 3 (Stage I whole-chain warmup for 5 epochs, then 3 more epochs of CDR-50% masking); for ESM C it's a clean Stage I CDR-50% run directly from BASE at epoch 4, no warmup at all \[same source: "The selected models were ESM2 Stage II CDR epoch 3 and ESM C Stage I CDR epoch 4, hereafter referred to as AbCDR-ESM2 and AbCDR-ESMC, respectively."\]. So the ESM C result is a genuinely clean CDR-only-vs-whole-chain comparison, while the ESM2-3B result is really WC-warmup-then-CDR beating WC-only, the paper never tests clean CDR-only on ESM2-3B, which is part of what our own ablation will check. The paper argues the cross-antigen and cross-backbone consistency shows the effect is a general recognition principle, not a target-specific artifact \[same source, Results §2.3.2\].  
\-       Caveat: on their two smallest datasets (Trastuzumab, N=422, and anti-H1 Hemagglutinin, N=1,038), whole-chain masking matched or outperformed CDR-focused masking, and the authors attribute this explicitly to insufficient N to be confident in the result, not to CDR masking failing to generalize \[same source, §2.3.3\]. This is a warning that our masking-strategy comparison could show noisy/reversed results in our own low-N regime, and shouldn't be over-interpreted from a single dataset.  
   
**Stopping rule for evotuning**  
   
The original plan was to pick the evotuning checkpoint using Spearman correlation on a small held-out slice of DMS (C05) data. The problem: that lets the evotuned model peek at extra labeled data beyond what it sees during DPO, which isn't a fair comparison against vanilla ESM2 (which never sees any DMS labels before its own DPO training).  
   
So instead, we want a purely self-supervised stopping rule, no DMS fitness labels at all, following the CDR-aware masking paper's approach. Their region-stratified logic (framework vs. CDR recovery) is defined via IMGT annotation on a single chain, so it doesn't actually require paired data, which means it transfers to our setup without much adaptation.  
   
Two candidate rules are worth comparing here: aggregate perplexity, and the paper's own region-stratified Pareto-knee rule. The paper only tested the second one, they never actually ran the comparison against plain perplexity, so we don't know from their results whether Pareto-knee is really better in general, only that they argue it should be. We'll ablate both ourselves.  
   
Aggregate perplexity (or MLM loss) alone is a bit risky as a stopping criterion, because it can keep improving on the evotuning validation set even after the checkpoint that's actually most useful for downstream tasks has passed. They saw exactly this in their unpaired-pretraining ablation: validation loss on the unpaired set kept improving with more training, while downstream (paired) performance had already peaked earlier, so they picked the earlier checkpoint (0.3 epoch) specifically to avoid overfitting to the broader corpus's distribution rather than just taking the loss-minimizing one \[Talaei, Walker et al., 2025 — Methods §4.2.3: "We observed validation loss on the unpaired set continued to improve while paired validation performance peaked early, so we chose the early (0.3) checkpoint to mitigate overfitting to the unpaired distribution."\]. Following what I said earlier about peaking at extra data we should avoid doing this and find a metric to trust.  
The alternative is a region-stratified masked-recovery Pareto-knee rule, entirely self-supervised: pick the earliest checkpoint that maximizes CDR-region masked-recovery accuracy, subject to framework-region recovery not dropping by more than 0.1 percentage points relative to a reference checkpoint \[same source, Methods §4.2.4: "choose the earliest epoch e\* that maximizes CDR(avg) subject to |FR(avg)Stage-I-e5 − FR(avg)Stage-II-e| ≤ 0.1 percentage points", where FR(avg) and CDR(avg) denote mean token-level MLM accuracy within FR and CDR regions, respectively\]. In their pipeline the reference point is the Stage I (whole-chain) checkpoint at epoch 5; since we won't always have a two-stage curriculum, the natural adaptation for us is to measure degradation relative to the base pretrained model instead.  
This rule isn't just theoretically clean, they checked it retrospectively against actual downstream performance too: whether the checkpoint their unsupervised rule picked lined up with the checkpoint that actually maximized downstream binding-regression performance. It did, reasonably well, which is why they conclude it's a sensible strategy for unsupervised model selection \["This validates our Pareto approach as a sensible unsupervised strategy for model selection." — same source, §2.3.3\].  
   
**DPO pair construction**  
   
Goal: a pair-construction rule that doesn't depend on the specific score distribution of whichever DMS dataset we're using, so it transfers across datasets without re-tuning.  
   
Current pairing uses a "delta\_based" strategy: each sequence's score is its M22 binding enrichment relative to WT. Three of the four pairing components (within\_pos, within\_neg, cross) are built with a uniform-rank-gap function, gap=0.5, so pairing there only depends on relative rank, already satisfying the goal above. The fourth component, wt\_anchors, and a global min\_score\_margin filter (0.1) break that though: they rely on fixed absolute thresholds (strong\_pos\_threshold=1.0, strong\_neg\_threshold=-5.0) tuned to C05's score scale. Those would need to be re-tuned per dataset as they stand, so we should rewrite them as relative/percentile-based cutoffs instead of fixed absolute values.  
   
Most of this comes from ProteinDPO. They tried several ways of building pairs from scalar fitness labels: random pairing then ordering by scalar value, prioritizing small-difference pairs, and prioritizing large-difference pairs. They landed on a "gap level" heuristic instead, a parameter in \[0,1\] that controls how far apart two paired sequences are in rank position rather than raw scalar value, gap level 1 places the maximum possible ranking distance between paired sequences, 0 the minimum, kept uniform across all pairs. Across a sweep of gap-level values most choices performed comparably, but 0.5 came out best on their validation data, exactly the value we use \[Widatalla et al., 2024 — https://www.biorxiv.org/content/10.1101/2024.05.20.595026v1.full — Figure S1 caption / Methods "Input construction": "we found a gap level of 0.5 performed the best on validation data"\]. They also tested three DPO objective variants: simple pairwise (Bradley-Terry) preference, ranked/listwise (Plackett-Luce, an ordered set rather than a single pair per example), and weighted (using the full continuous fitness score rather than a binarized preference) \[same source, Figure S1 caption\]. We currently use the weighted variant.  
   
g-DPO takes a different approach to pair construction: it clusters mutants by shared mutational positions (union-mask clustering) and samples pairs within those clusters, mainly to cut down the quadratic blowup in pair count for large datasets, rather than to make pairing dataset-independent \[g-DPO, Related Work §2.1 and Methods §3.1\]. We don't use that part, the gap-level rank pairing above is entirely ProteinDPO's.  
   
We've experimented with both full DPO and LoRA DPO. The working hypothesis is that LoRA should help more in the low-data regime specifically: DPO tends to overfit easily with only a handful of training pairs, and LoRA constrains how much the model's weights can move, which should act as a natural regularizer there.  
   
   
**Low-data regime: fixed N vs. percentage of dataset**  
   
Proposed: fixed, log-spaced N (as already used for C05: \~50, 200, 2k, 4k, 9k, 20k, ...) as the primary reported axis, not percentage of the dataset.  
   
Rationale: our candidate DMS datasets vary enormously in total size (a few hundred to tens of thousands, based on the datasets surveyed in the section below). A percentage-based axis makes the same nominal point (e.g., "10%") correspond to very different absolute training-set sizes across datasets, which would make direct comparison across datasets harder to read off a shared plot, and would require converting percentages to absolute N anyway to find a comparable "crossover point." We can still compute and report percentage in a table alongside N per dataset, since readers may reasonably want to know how "low-data" a given N really is relative to that dataset's total size.  
   
For the DMS side, subsampling currently uses stratified quantile-binning (10 bins), with n allocated proportionally across bins with largest-remainder rounding, then sampled uniformly within each bin, which guarantees positive examples survive even at very small N. {STILL OPEN: is stratified subsampling actually the right call here, or should this just be plain random sampling instead? Random sampling could simulate better a in vitro experiment in which we don’t have any certainty about having stratified data} Validation and test splits are never subsampled either way, only the training pool shrinks with N, so held-out evaluation stays constant across the whole curve.
